Courses Taken

Courses


Fundamentals of Computer Science (Math121)

Introductory computer science. Topics including elementary algorithms and data structures, functional and procedural abstraction, data abstraction, object orientation, logic, and the digital representations of numbers.
Fundamentals of Computer Science II (Math221)

Second course in computer science. Topics include digital design, from gates to processors; the construction of interpreters, including language parsing and run-time systems; parallelism and concurrency; and universality.

Computer Systems (Math389)

A study of the design and implementation of computing systems. A survey of computer architecture and the hardware-software interface, compilation and run time, and concurrent and networked programming including consensus algorithms.

Algorithms and Data Structures (Math382)

An in-depth study of the design and analysis of algorithms and data structures. Methods for solving combinatorial problems are studied emphasizing an abstract approach.

Computability and Complexity (Math387)

Introduction to models of computation including finite automata, formal languages, and Turing machines, culminating in universality and undecidability. An introduction to resource-bounded models of computation and algorithmic complexity classes, including NP and PSPACE, and the notions of relative hardness and completeness.

Computer Graphics (Csci385)

Introduction to computer image synthesis and mathematical modeling for computer graphics applications. Topics include image processing, 2-D and 3-D modeling techniques such as curve and surface representation, geometric algorithms for intersection and hidden surface removal, 3-D rendering, and animation.
Operating Systems Design and Implementation (Csci392)

This course covers the low-level details of the software that drives computing hardware, spanning such disparate systems as supercomputers, the Internet backbone, laptops, and smartphones. Topics include kernel architectures, scheduling, memory management, security policies and mechanisms, assurance, file systems, networking, virtualization, real time, safety-critical and security-critical systems. Several operating system components are implemented.
Artificial Intelligence (Csci377)

An introduction to the construction of software systems that emulate intelligent behavior. Topics include knowledge representation, reasoning under uncertainty, logic, planning, and algorithmic strategies for large-scale combinatorial search.

Introduction to Analysis (Math112)

An introduction to formal mathematics. Field axioms, the real and complex fields, sequences and series. Complex functions, continuity and differentiation; power series, the complex exponential and complex dynamical systems.
Discrete Structures (Math113)

Sets, cardinality, number theory, combinatorics, probability. Proof techniques and problem solving. Additional topics may include graph theory, finite fields, and computer experimentation.

Linear Algebra (Math201)

A course in linear algebra. An introduction to field structures, followed by presentation of the algebraic theory of finite dimensional vector spaces. Topics include linear transformations, determinants, eigenvalues, eigenvectors, diagonalization. Geometry of inner product spaces is examined in the setting of real and complex fields. Applications studied include Leontieff’s input-output model, computer graphics, difference and differential equations, Markov chains and statistical applications.

Vector Calculus (Math202)

A course in multivariable calculus. The derivative as a linear function, partial derivatives, optimization, Taylor series, multiple integrals, change of variables, Stokes' theorem.

Abstract Algebra (Math332)

An elementary treatment of the algebraic structure of groups, rings, fields, and/or algebras. (Audited)

Probability (Math391)

Begins with first principles, builds notions of independence, conditioning, random variables, and explores the properties of the fundamental discrete and continuous distributions. Special topics may include Markov chains, stochastic processes, and measure-theoretic development of probability theory.
General Physics 1&2 (Phys101,102)

Calculus-based introduction to the classical mechanics of particles and systems—kinematics, laws of motion, conservation principles, rotational dynamics, oscillators, electricity and magnetism, optics, and other topics at the discretion of the instructor.
Oscillations and Waves (Phys201)

A course in oscillations, waves and mathematical methods. Damped and driven vibrations, coupled oscillators, and waves. Related mathematical methods are introduced: complex numbers, ordinary differential equations, linear algebra, and Fourier analysis. Weekly laboratories provide an introduction to electronics.
Modern Physics (Phys202)

Introduction to thermal physics, special relativity, and quantum mechanics, with applications to atomic, nuclear, condensed matter, and particle physics as time permits. Weekly laboratories include an introduction to computational physics, the Millikan oil drop experiment, measurement of the speed of light, determination of Planck’s constant, the charge-to-mass ratio of the electron, and blackbody radiation.
Computational Mathods (Phys367)

This course focuses on diverse physical problems and computational techniques that can be applied to them, with an emphasis on the mathematical motivation behind the methods. Problems are drawn from electrodynamics, quantum mechanics, classical mechanics, and special and general relativity. The course develops methods for solving ODEs and PDEs and integrating arbitrary functions in multiple dimensions. Numerical linear algebra is covered in both full and iterative form. Additional topics include nonlinear minimization, Galerkin methods, neural network models, and chaotic dynamics.
Logic (Phil201)

An introduction to the formal logic of propositions, identity, and quantification, which include metalogic, philosophy of logic and applying formal logic when evaluating real arguments.
Computation (Phil412)

Surveys the important epistemological roles of computation in philosophy and science, especially concerning complex systems. The course surveys computation theory, and it includes computer laboratory exercises involving programming and computer simulations. Key course topics are illustrated with case studies, such as cellular automata, artificial life, and intelligent robot scientists.
